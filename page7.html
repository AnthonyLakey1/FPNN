Consuming Tea May Reduce the Risk of Early Death From Heart Disease

Author

Anthony Lakey

Published

November 18, 2022

Close

Black tea is known to those who need a caffeine boost. What if tea not only increases your energy, but may help you later down the road?

According to a 2014 observational study in the United Kingdom (UK), which included data from half a million people, those who consumed two or more cups of tea a day showed a 9-13% lower risk of an early death from cardiovascular disease or stroke, compared with non-tea drinkers.

Research also concluded that it did not matter if consumers used milk or sugar in their tea. Drinking coffee also did not show any signs to weaken this affect. 

Researchers provided participants with a survey that asked about lifestyle, demographics, and other health related questions. Researchers followed participants for the 14-year study period, until early 2020. In the event that a participant passed away, the researchers obtained date and cause of death from the UK National Health Service.

The researchers found a 9-13% lower risk of death among people who drank at least 2 cups of tea per day than among non-tea drinkers. Tea drinking was associated with reduced mortality from cardiovascular diseases and stroke—but not any reduction from cancer or respiratory disease than non-tea drinkers.

Researchers caution that the study is observational and cannot prove that tea drinking lowered the risk of death directly. They also did not take into account some aspects of tea drinking, such as cup size and tea strength, which may be important. 

Types of tea used in this study:
- Green Tea
- Black Tea

Tea is also popular amongst those who have common cold symptoms. While there are no direct evidence that hot tea actually helps a sore throat, many say that it does in fact help. Which is why it does not come as a surprise that tea may help other types of illnesses.

So next time you need a little more energy, think about drinking tea.
